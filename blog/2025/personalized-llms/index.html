Why personalized LLM?

Generative AI models are usually trained on a large amount of data, that can have general capabilities but not specific to a particular user. For example, a general-purpose LLM can generate text (the task) about a topic but not follow specific style or tone to a specific user.

Personalized LLMs, on the other hand, aim to tailor the model to a specific user, in the following aspects:

  • Style and Tone - Preferences, for example, adapting the style and tone of the LLM to a specific user, which can be very helpful for specific tasks/usecases, such as education, customer service, etc.
  • Personal Knowledge, for example, an agentic LLM with personal knowledge of a specific user can act as a personal assistant.
  • Specific Task/Knowledge, for example, a LLM with specific knowledge of a specific task can be used to solve a specific task, such as a medical LLM for medical diagnosis.

In general, personalized LLMs can be used in the two following scenarios:

  • Cat A: LLMs as Personal Assistants - with user-specific knowledge, LLMs can act as a personal assistant to help users with their specific tasks (Digital twin), i.e., rather than the user does a task A on their own (the task requires user’s knowledge or skills or information), the LLM can do it for them.
  • Cat B: LLMs as Personal Models, i.e., rather than the model provides a general answer/support to a user, the model provides a personalized answer/support to the user based on the user’s preferences.

Based on the above definition, we can categorize the following usecases (that I borrowed from 1) to better understand the potential of personalized LLMs:

  • Personalized Recommendation aim to recommend items to a user that match their preferences (Cat B)
  • Personalized Search aim to understand the user’s historical search behavior and preferences, and then provide a personalized search result (Cat B)
  • Personalized Healthcare can be either Cat A or Cat B, depending on the specific task, e.g., calling emergency service, doctor’s appointment (Cat A) or medical diagnosis with special medical knowledge (Cat B)
  • Software Development - with user-specific knowledge (developer’s knowledge, project goals, etc.) to help/replace the developer in the development process (Cat A)

How to inject user-specific knowledge into LLMs?

There are several methods for injecting personal information or knowledge into LLMs to achieve personalization:

  • Prompting and In-Context Learning: Incorporating directly into the input prompt. For instance, in recommendation systems, a user’s historical data can be included in the prompt to generate tailored suggestions. Similarly, in personalized education, a student’s affective state, cognitive state and learning style can be incorporated into the prompt. However, this method faces the context length limitation of LLMs or the higher inference cost [5].
  • Fine-tuning: Fine-tuning the LLM on a specific personal dataset/task, usually via PEFT (like LoRA, etc.). However, this method requires a large amount of personal data, which is not always available. Fine-tuning also faces overfitting issue or significant performance degradation on original tasks.
  • Retrieval-Augmented Methods: RAG based methods store personal data in a vector/embedding based database, and the retrieve the most relevant data based on the user’s query. While this approach can handle a longer context user data, it may face the challenges of potential information loss or noisy retrieval [6].

How to evaluate the performance of personalized LLMs?

Similar to the evaluation of LLMs, the evaluation of personalized LLMs is also challenging, especially when it comes to preference-based tasks, i.e., how to quatitatively measure tone, style of a LLM response [4].

Safety and Privacy

As discussed in [1], fine-tuning LLMs with personal data can lead to potential safety and privacy issues, such as removing the original alignment of the LLM (personalization as jailbreak technique), or suffering from personalization bias when the personal data is not representative of the general population. If the data to train the LLM has been collected to train the LLM, it may also be leaked via adversarial attacks as shown in [2,3].
